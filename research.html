Field of study

My research focuses on the remote sensing of volcanic particles from ground- and satellite-based systems using physical-electromagnetic models and statistical learning theory. I mainly develop advanced algorithms for the microphysical modelling of volcanic clouds, observed by satellite radiometers in the Thermal-Infrared and Microwave spectrum regions.

Sample of my work:

The objective of studying volcanic clouds is to prevent damages to the population and the infrastuctures and to identify no-fly zones for aviation.

This short example shows how I retrieve physical properties of ash particles during an ongoing volcanic eruption at Mt. Etna (Italy) the 24th November 2006. I implemented a forward-model (Romeo et al. 2025, 2023) to simulate the spectral response of volcanic particles observed by satellite radiometers. Figure 1 shows the simulated spectral response for this specific eruption, assuming an omogeneous layer between 6 and 7 km altitudes. The arched curves represent different particles effective radius (μm), while the vertical dashed lines represent different optical depths. The colorbar displays different concentrations (mg m-3) of ash particles.

From the simulations it is possible to retrieve properties of the volcanic clouds (inversion technique) as the ash concentration (mg m-3) and effective radius (μm). The two figures below show the estimated ash concentration and effective radius for the volcanic cloud observed by the Aqua-MODIS spectro-radiometer over the Sicily (Italy).

These two figures show how the Catania airport (Fontanarossa) and the close city of Catania are affected by the fallout of ash particles with medium concentration levels (according to ICAO standards). This entailed the closure of the airport during the eruption.
